What this is really about

Most of your week isn't the work you got into this for.

A significant share of what fills a product manager's week is structural: keeping things moving, not deciding what's right. As AI takes on that layer, what comes into focus is exactly what drew most of us to this work in the first place.

Today: structural overhead

Writing tickets so engineering has what it needs
Driving alignment across stakeholders
Synthesizing a week of notes into a document
Chasing status updates across the team

With AI: the work you love

Deep thinking about what users actually need
Sharp problem definition and prioritization
High-quality decisions with genuine reasoning
Seeing your idea ship and land with real users

Four stages where everything changes

A PM's work, reimagined

Here is what product work actually looks like at each stage when AI is part of how it gets done. Not what changes in theory, but what changes in practice.

01

Stage one

Understanding
the problem

"You walk in with a problem statement you can genuinely stand behind."

Without AI

–
Schedule three separate conversations
–
Wait for data to be pulled and investigated
–
Move forward on a thin foundation
–
Rely on instinct to fill the gaps

With AI

✓
Surface patterns across all sources at once
✓
See where evidence is strong vs thin
✓
Get alternative explanations you hadn't considered
✓
Ask questions you might not have thought to ask
When you can surface patterns, test alternative explanations, and see where your evidence is thin before you start building, you stop making decisions on a thin foundation. You arrive at discovery with a problem statement you can actually defend.
02

Stage two

Deciding
what to build

"Requirements that were never separated from the conversation that produced them."

The old process

–
Write spec in isolation, circulate and wait
–
Cycle through back-and-forth on questions
–
Quality limited to questions that got asked
–
Experiment design comes later, if at all

With AI

✓
Agent listens to the conversation and drafts live
✓
Flags edge cases, NFRs, and policy questions
✓
Notes reasoning behind decisions, not just outcomes
✓
Experiment design built in from the start
When requirements draft themselves in real time and experiment design is built in from the start, you show up to build already aligned on what success looks like rather than still trying to get there.
03

Stage three

Building
and refining

"Is this what we meant? Answering that is a shared act."

Traditional execution

–
Spec handed to engineering, build in a black box
–
Review reveals gaps after the fact
–
PM and engineer work from a shared doc
–
Iteration takes days to weeks

With AI

✓
Coding agent produces commented drafts
✓
Reasoning visible alongside the output
✓
PM + engineer review intent together
✓
Revised code returns in hours, not days
When reasoning is visible alongside the output, you and your engineer stop reviewing code in a black box and start reviewing intent together. You catch things neither of you would have caught alone.
04

Stage four

Learning
and iterating

"What used to take a sprint to learn is now happening in days."

Before

–
Manual dashboard checks and waits
–
Wait for analyst to confirm results
–
Sprint-length learning cycles
–
Launch = end of delivery

With AI

✓
Continuous monitoring against guardrails
✓
Anomalies caught before they spread
✓
Day-level iteration cycles
✓
Launch = beginning of learning
When anomalies surface before they spread and iterations cycle in days rather than sprints, shipping stops being the end of the process and becomes the beginning of learning.

What this requires from you

Five key shifts

As AI takes on more of the structural layer, the premium shifts almost entirely to the quality of thinking and decision-making. Here's what that means in practice.

01

The premium on good judgment

Which problem is worth solving, and which one just feels urgent? When two reasonable options are on the table, which do you choose, and what does your reasoning say about what you believe? AI can inform but cannot answer these questions.

02

From generation to curation

When a tool can produce multiple versions of a spec or several framings of a research synthesis, the bottleneck stops being the ability to produce things and starts being the ability to judge them with genuine rigor.

03

Systems thinking and orchestration

Your role begins to resemble something closer to an architect than a coordinator. One agent synthesizes research, another drafts requirements, another generates code, another monitors post-launch signal. You own how they fit together.

04

Speed, iteration, and risk

Faster delivery cycles remove the natural checkpoints that slower processes gave you for free: time to reconsider an assumption before it became a shipped feature. You have to build that reflection in yourself.

05

Autonomy, accountability, and the governance instinct

Governance is becoming a core product responsibility rather than a compliance afterthought. It covers the decisions about how AI systems are designed, constrained, and monitored within your product. Both trace back to the people who defined the goals and chose to deploy the system. The instinct to ask "what could go wrong, and for whom?" needs to be present from the beginning of the work.

What doesn't change

Some things AI simply cannot reach.

AI can accelerate research, draft requirements, generate code, and surface anomalies. What it cannot do is replace the instincts that have always defined great product management, including genuine curiosity about people, the ability to make a call when the path is unclear, and the trust built through how you show up for the people around you.

User empathy

Understanding what someone actually needs, not what they say they need or what the data suggests, requires sitting with ambiguity about motivation and behavior. AI doesn't have access to the context this depends on.

Strategic clarity

Absorbing uncertainty and identifying the right direction when competing priorities pull in different ways. Those moments aren't going anywhere.

Trust

The relationships you build with engineers, designers, stakeholders, and users. AI can support the quality of your decisions. It cannot build the relationships that make them land.

The work ahead is more interesting than what came before.

The overhead that has consumed so much of our time and energy is beginning to lift, and what tends to replace it is a version of this work that is more strategic, more creative, and more directly focused on the things that have always made great product management valuable. Here is where to start.

01

Adopt a growth mindset toward AI

Start using AI tools in your daily work. Try new things. Some won't work, and that's part of the process. The goal is to build instinct through experience, and experience only comes from doing.

02

Find your people

Identify colleagues or peers you trust who are on the same journey. They don't need to be ahead of you. What matters is a shared willingness to learn, experiment, and compare notes along the way.

03

Know where you stand

Take an honest look at your current AI fluency and identify where the gaps are. The AI Fluency Framework for Product Managers is designed to help you do exactly that. Use it as a starting point, then make a plan.
