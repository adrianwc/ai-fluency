What this is really about

Most of your week isn't the work you got into this for.

A significant share of what fills a product manager's week is structural — keeping things moving, not deciding what's right. As AI takes on that layer, what comes into focus is exactly what drew most of us to this work in the first place.

Today — structural overhead

Writing tickets so engineering has what it needs
Driving alignment across stakeholders
Synthesizing a week of notes into a document
Chasing status updates across the team

With AI — the work you love

Deep thinking about what users actually need
Sharp problem definition and prioritization
High-quality decisions with genuine reasoning
Seeing your idea ship and land with real users

Four stages where everything changes

A PM's work — reimagined

Abstract descriptions of change are easy to read past. Here's what each stage of the work actually feels like when AI is part of how it gets done.

What this requires from you

Five key shifts

As AI takes on more of the structural layer, the premium shifts almost entirely to the quality of thinking and decision-making. Here's what that means in practice.

01

The premium on good judgment

Which problem is worth solving — and which one just feels urgent? When two reasonable options are on the table, which do you choose, and what does your reasoning say about what you believe? AI can inform but cannot answer these questions.

02

From generation to curation

When a tool can produce multiple versions of a spec or several framings of a research synthesis, the bottleneck stops being the ability to produce things and starts being the ability to judge them with genuine rigor.

03

Systems thinking and orchestration

Your role begins to resemble something closer to an architect than a coordinator. One agent synthesizes research, another drafts requirements, another generates code, another monitors post-launch signal. You own how they fit together.

04

Speed, iteration, and risk

Faster delivery cycles remove the natural checkpoints that slower processes gave you for free — time to reconsider an assumption before it became a shipped feature. You have to build that reflection in yourself.

05

Autonomy, accountability, and the governance instinct

Governance — the decisions about how AI systems are designed, constrained, and monitored within your product — is becoming a core product responsibility rather than a compliance afterthought. An automated workflow that produces a biased outcome, a feature that erodes user trust: these trace back to the people who defined the goals and chose to deploy the system. The instinct to ask "what could go wrong, and for whom?" needs to be present from the beginning of the work.

What doesn't change

Some things AI simply cannot reach.

The ability to understand what someone actually needs — not what they say, not what the data suggests — requires being curious about people in a way that depends entirely on human context. That kind of work has always separated good product managers from great ones.

User empathy

Understanding what someone actually needs — not what they say they need, not what the data suggests — requires sitting with ambiguity about motivation and behavior. AI doesn't have access to the context this depends on.

Strategic clarity

Looking at competing priorities and identifying the right direction — absorbing uncertainty and making a call when the path forward isn't obvious. Those moments aren't going anywhere.

Trust

The relationships you build with engineers, designers, stakeholders, and users. AI can support the quality of your decisions. It cannot build the relationships that make them land.

The work ahead is more interesting than what came before.

01

Adopt a growth mindset toward AI

Start using AI tools in your daily work. Try new things. Some won't work, and that's part of the process. The goal is to build instinct through experience, and experience only comes from doing.

02

Find your people

Identify colleagues or peers you trust who are on the same journey. They don't need to be ahead of you. What matters is a shared willingness to learn, experiment, and compare notes along the way.

03

Know where you stand

Take an honest look at your current AI fluency and identify where the gaps are. The AI Fluency Framework for Product Managers is designed to help you do exactly that. Use it as a starting point, then make a plan.

The overhead that has consumed so much of our time and energy is beginning to lift — and what tends to replace it is a version of this work that is more strategic, more creative, and more directly focused on the things that have always made great product management valuable.
