About the Framework

A portable core for how PMs work, not just what they build.

This framework defines the skills product managers and product executives need to thrive as AI agents become integrated into how product work gets done. It is organized into six domains covering the full scope of modern PM responsibilities, with skills differentiated by seniority level — designed as a living reference for career development and organizational capability building.

Why this framework exists

The bottleneck is moving.

Product management has always required holding a lot of things together at once: writing requirements, synthesizing research, driving alignment. These are real contributions. They are also, increasingly, the parts of the job that AI is becoming capable of handling. As that structural layer shifts, the work that has always been hardest to do well becomes more consequential.

01

The premium on good judgment

As AI takes on more of the structural layer, the premium shifts almost entirely to the quality of thinking and decision-making. Which problem is worth solving, and which one just feels urgent? When two reasonable options are on the table, which do you choose, and what does your reasoning say about what you believe? These are questions AI can inform but cannot answer — they require judgment grounded in business context and connected to a clear sense of what the product is trying to accomplish.

02

From generation to curation

When a tool can produce multiple versions of a spec, several framings of a research synthesis, or a range of approaches to a prioritization problem, the bottleneck stops being the ability to produce those things and starts being the ability to judge them. Which framing is right? Where is the AI's reasoning sound, and where has it missed something only someone with deeper context would catch? Those who can evaluate AI output with genuine rigor will consistently produce better outcomes than those who accept the first plausible result.

03

Systems thinking and orchestration

As AI agents take on more execution work, your role begins to resemble something closer to an architect than a coordinator. Each agent has a defined scope — one synthesizes research, another drafts requirements, another generates code, another monitors post-launch signal. Your job is to understand how those agents relate to each other, where the handoffs happen, and where gaps or conflicts can emerge. When the mechanical work is largely automated, the quality of your thinking about how the system is designed — and how you intervene when something goes wrong — is what determines whether the work produces the right outcome.

04

Speed, iteration, and the relationship with risk

Faster delivery cycles create real opportunity, and they also change your relationship with risk. Slower processes gave you something valuable alongside their frustrations: time between a decision and its consequences, time to reconsider an assumption before it became a shipped feature. When cycle times compress, those natural checkpoints disappear — and you have to build that deliberate reflection in yourself. The PMs who navigate this well make confident decisions knowing the information is incomplete, and treat each release as the beginning of a learning cycle rather than the end of a delivery one.

05

Autonomy, accountability, and the governance instinct

As AI systems take on more autonomous actions within product workflows, your accountability expands in ways that are not always obvious at first. Governance — the decisions about how AI systems are designed, constrained, and monitored within your product — is becoming a core product responsibility rather than a compliance afterthought. An automated workflow that produces a biased outcome, a feature that erodes user trust, a release that creates a compliance issue: these trace back to the people who defined the goals, set the parameters, and chose to deploy the system. The instinct to ask what could go wrong, and for whom, needs to be present from the beginning of the work.

How to use this framework

Differentiated by seniority, built for all PM levels.

The framework contains six domains, each covering a distinct area of PM responsibility. Within each domain, individual skills are described along with guidance on which seniority level the skill is most critical for. Use these definitions to calibrate your focus.

Level 1

Mid PM

3–7 years experience

Skills focused on execution, agentic workflow proficiency, and day-to-day data and delivery capabilities. Building the habits and tool fluency that form the foundation of everything else.

Level 2

Senior PM

7+ years experience

Skills that span execution and strategy, including cross-domain thinking, storytelling, and research leadership. Operating at the intersection of delivery and direction.

Level 3

Exec

Directors, VPs, CPO

Skills focused on organizational design, governance, talent strategy, and AI investment decisions. Responsible for how the team operates and how it grows, not just what it ships.

Note on domain expertise: Domain knowledge specific to your team — such as ad tech knowledge, platform experience, or content domain fluency — is intentionally excluded from this core framework. It should be applied as a seventh domain overlay alongside these six. This framework covers how PMs work, not necessarily what they build.
